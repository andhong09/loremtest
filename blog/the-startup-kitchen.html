The Startup Kitchen

It's a classic story -- the startup founders who eat ramen noodles while they work around the clock to build their brand-new company. Sam, Jacob, and I were leaving full time jobs to launch a company, and were ready to pay our founder ramen dues... until we began looking at our kitchen as a startup.

Quoting Eric Ries, "A startup is a human institution designed to create a new product or service under conditions of extreme uncertainty." In this case, the startup kitchen is the same thing, except instead of a product, it creates food and you are your own customer.

Like a young company, a startup kitchen operates with a strict and highly frugal budget. Every dollar spent on food is a dollar that is not spent on something else. Just like any other business expense, we aim to maximize the output of each dollar spent. Overspending on food is obviously an inefficient way to deploy capital, but every organization may see a different output per dollar spent on food. What are the productivity gains?

We know that the brain consumes 20% of our resting metabolic rate, and that a good balance of calories can help keep you focused. Beyond the obvious, good food is a morale booster. In the early days, a startup feels like family, so coming together around the dinner table is important.

But are we wasting time while eating? Actually, dinner is our favorite meeting of the day. We take a step back, talk about blockers, goals, strategies, and plans. If cooking with your co-founders sounds like agony and your dinners dissolve into arguments, stop reading and maybe get some new co-founders.

Feedback Loop

When we finish cooking, we sit around the same table and eat. The chefs taste the meal, and the rest of the team will typically say "this is good" or "this is not good" at some point. Every grocery bill is accounted for, and under-purchased or over-purchased food is noticed.

Each trip to the kitchen has been informed by the previous night's feedback and experience. More than the meal, we're always trying to optimize portion size, timing, and clean-up. The chef is "dogfooding" -- using the product (eating the food) constantly. Trips to the market are iterative, learning from the previous week. In other words, the kitchen startup has a tight feedback loop.

Teamwork

There are a certain number of tasks that go into a dinner. The team has become naturally specialized in different tasks, which helps the job get done as best as possible, and helps us avoid reassigning jobs each night. Sam typically decides what we're cooking and chops. I cook the meat and tame the stove. Jacob is our master cleaner. On trips to the market, we divide and conquer, often completing a week of shopping in 10-15 minutes.

While we know the drill, we're also always picking up slack. In the kitchen you're highly attuned to the other chefs. You're constantly aware of timing, who needs an extra hand, and how you are making things more efficient or less efficient. Like a production line, you're avoiding downstream bottlenecks and reducing waste whenever possible.

While cooking, the team is making numerous small decisions -- consciously or unconsciously to get the job done. When and what are we cooking? What jobs are involved? Who is doing what? Are we roasting or sautéing? Most of the time we agree. When we disagree, we let the person who cares the most make the decision.

Takeaway

Cook with your team -- there's a lot more to it than just food.
